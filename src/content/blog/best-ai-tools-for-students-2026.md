## Important: Academic Integrity

Using AI tools doesn't have to violate academic integrity — but it can if you're not careful.

**Generally acceptable:**
- Using AI to brainstorm and outline (then writing yourself)
- Grammar and spell checking with Grammarly
- Summarizing papers for your own understanding
- Debugging code in CS courses (check your course policy)
- Generating flashcards for studying

**Check your syllabus before:**
- Submitting AI-generated text as your own work
- Using AI for take-home exams
- Having AI complete problem sets

**Bottom line:** AI is a tool. A calculator doesn't do your math homework — it does arithmetic so you can focus on math. Use AI the same way.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is using AI tools cheating?**
A: Not inherently. Using Grammarly to fix grammar has been accepted for decades. Using ChatGPT to write your essay and submitting it as your own work is a different story. Know your institution's policies and use AI ethically.

**Q: Will AI tools get me in trouble with plagiarism detectors?**
A: AI detectors (Turnitin, GPTZero) are improving but still imperfect. The safest approach: use AI for research and brainstorming, write in your own voice. Heavily AI-generated text stylistically reads differently and instructors notice.

**Q: What's the best free AI tool for students?**
A: ChatGPT free (GPT-4o mini) for general tasks. GitHub Copilot (completely free with .edu) for CS students. Notion AI with the free education plan for organization.

**Q: Do I need to pay for AI tools?**
A: The free tiers of most tools are genuinely useful. Start free, upgrade only if you hit real limitations. ChatGPT Plus at $20/month is the most defensible paid upgrade — the jump from GPT-4o mini to GPT-4o is significant for complex work.

**Q: What AI tools do medical students use?**
