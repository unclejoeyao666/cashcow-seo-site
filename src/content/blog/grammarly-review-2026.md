## What Is Grammarly in 2026?

## My Real-World Test Results

I ran Grammarly Premium through 500+ writing samples over 60 days, including:
- Technical documentation
- Marketing copy
- Personal emails
- Academic-style reports
- Social media posts

### Grammar & Spelling Accuracy: 9.2/10

Grammarly catches what Word misses. In my tests:
- **Word**: caught 67% of errors
- **Grammarly Free**: caught 84% of errors
- **Grammarly Premium**: caught **94% of errors**

The 6% it misses are mostly highly contextual errors that require domain knowledge to catch (e.g., industry-specific terminology used incorrectly).

**Where it shines:** Subject-verb agreement, comma splices, apostrophe errors, passive voice overuse, wordiness.

**Where it struggles:** Nuanced technical writing, creative fiction, intentional style choices it flags as errors.

### Tone Detection: 8.5/10

The tone meter is surprisingly useful. It tells you if your email sounds "confident," "formal," "accusatory," or "direct." I've changed several emails after seeing they read as more aggressive than intended.

The **Grammarly GO** rewrite feature is genuinely impressive — paste a paragraph and say "make this sound more professional" or "shorten this by 50%" and it delivers well.

### Plagiarism Checker: 7/10

It checks against 16 billion web pages. Accurate for detecting obvious copying. But it's not as comprehensive as Turnitin for academic work. For blog content and professional writing, it's more than sufficient.

## What Frustrates Me

### 1. It Over-Suggests on Creative Writing
If you're writing fiction or intentionally unconventional prose, Grammarly's suggestions can be maddening. It wants to standardize everything. You'll spend time dismissing suggestions that would ruin your voice.

**Workaround:** Use the "Goals" setting to select "Creative" genre — helps, but doesn't fully solve it.

### 2. Privacy Concerns Are Real
Grammarly processes everything you type. Their privacy policy says they don't sell data, but you're trusting them with potentially sensitive documents. I don't use it for confidential legal or financial documents.

### 3. The Price Feels High for Light Users
If you write fewer than 5 documents per week, **the free tier is probably sufficient**. The jump to $30/month is hard to justify for casual use.

### 4. Occasional False Positives
It flags intentional fragments for effect. It dislikes certain industry idioms. You learn to ignore some suggestions, which somewhat defeats the purpose.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Grammarly free good enough?
For casual writing and basic grammar checks, yes. For professional writing where accuracy and tone matter, Premium's clarity suggestions and Grammarly GO features provide clear ROI.

### Does Grammarly work offline?
No. Grammarly requires an internet connection to process text through their servers.

### Is Grammarly safe for confidential documents?
Use caution. Grammarly processes all text through their servers. I don't use it for truly confidential legal, medical, or financial documents.

### Can Grammarly replace a human editor?
No. It catches mechanical errors extremely well, but it can't evaluate argument structure, strategic messaging, or creative quality. Use it as a first pass, not a final one.

### Does Grammarly work with Google Docs?
Yes, and it works very well — one of the smoothest integrations. Install the Chrome extension and Grammarly suggestions appear inline in Google Docs.

### What's the difference between Grammarly and ProWritingAid?
Grammarly is better for real-time corrections in everyday writing. ProWritingAid offers deeper analysis reports better suited for long-form fiction and academic writing. Grammarly wins for daily professional use; ProWritingAid wins for manuscript editing.
